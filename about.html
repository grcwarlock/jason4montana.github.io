About

Four generations in. No plans to leave.

My great-grandfather came to Lewistown in 1911 and became the town's first osteopath. My grandmother was born here in 1920. My grandfather worked as a mining engineer for the Anaconda Company, which is how my mother came into the world in Butte; Central Montana families have always followed the work, and they have always come back. That is the family I come from, and this is the place I am running to represent.

What I did before this campaign.

Spending years as an intelligence officer, first at the Defense Intelligence Agency and then at the Pentagon, trained me to do one thing exceptionally well: read what is actually happening versus what someone needs you to believe is happening. Threat analysis demands going to primary sources, pressure-testing the framing of everything you are handed, and being willing to name what you found clearly, however inconvenient the finding turns out to be for the people who sent you in. That discipline does not retire when you leave federal service.

What is heading toward Central Montana right now, in the form of outside infrastructure investment, legislative deals negotiated in Helena, and sustained economic pressure on multi-generational farm and ranch families, deserves exactly that kind of scrutiny. Bringing it to the state house is the specific thing this candidacy offers that neither party candidate can match.

The decisions that will shape what Central Montana looks like in twenty years are being made right now, mostly by people who do not live here and do not plan to. Having someone in Helena who knows how to read what is actually in those agreements, not just the press release version, is the whole point of running.

What brought this on.

Running AI and cybersecurity literacy workshops for neighbors in Lewistown started well before any of this did. People had real questions and deserved real answers, not the marketing version, and helping them understand what is actually happening with the technologies being deployed into their community felt like the right use of what I know. That same instinct drove the organized community response to a zoning variance last year: residents engaged the process in good faith, made legally grounded arguments through every available channel, and were overruled. Learning why that happened, specifically, and what tools exist at the state level to prevent it from repeating, is the kind of groundwork that does not make the paper. It does, however, translate directly into useful representation.

Watching outside interests make consequential decisions about Central Montana from a distance, without meaningful input from the people who actually live here, feels personal. That is not a liability in a representative. It is, arguably, the whole job description.

Why independent, and why this race.

Both party candidates will arrive in Helena carrying obligations that predate anything District 37 asked of them. Caucus relationships, state party leadership expectations, and donor networks operating well above the district level create real and ongoing pressure; that pressure runs in one direction, and it is not toward the Judith Basin. Running as an independent removes all of it. The accountability in this campaign runs horizontally, to the residents of this district, and nowhere else.

Four generations of this family have lived and worked and built things in Central Montana. Representing it in the state house is not a career move. It is the logical next thing.
